Here's how AT1 and Tier 2 fit into the regulatory capital structure under Basel III / CRR.

**The Pillar 1 minimums (% of RWA)**

CET1 ≥ 4.5%, Tier 1 (CET1 + AT1) ≥ 6.0%, Total Capital (Tier 1 + Tier 2) ≥ 8.0%.

"AT1 brings Tier 1 to 6.0%. Tier 2 (subordinated debt) brings Total Capital to 8.0%." This creates the implicit slot structure: AT1 can fill up to 1.5% of RWA (the gap between 4.5% and 6%), and Tier 2 can fill up to 2.0% of RWA (the gap between 6% and 8%).

**Substitution is one-way**

Higher-quality capital can always substitute for lower: excess CET1 counts toward Tier 1, and excess Tier 1 counts toward Total Capital. But the reverse is not allowed — AT1 cannot fill a CET1 shortfall, and Tier 2 cannot fill a Tier 1 shortfall. So a bank with 4% CET1 + 2% AT1 + 2% Tier 2 hits 8% Total Capital but is still in breach of the 4.5% CET1 minimum.

**Buffers are CET1-only**

On top of Pillar 1 sits the combined buffer requirement, which must be met entirely in CET1:
- Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5% (universal)
- Countercyclical Buffer: 0–2.5% (jurisdictional)
- G-SIB buffer: 1.0–3.5% (G-SIBs only)
- O-SII / D-SIB buffer: up to 3% in EU
- Systemic Risk Buffer: jurisdiction-set

"The 'combined capital buffer requirement' must be met with Common Equity Tier 1 capital ('CET1')". For a typical large EU bank the combined buffer adds roughly 3.5–5% CET1 on top of the 4.5% minimum. AT1 and Tier 2 do not help here.

**MDA — the enforcement mechanism**

If CET1 falls into the combined buffer zone, Maximum Distributable Amount restrictions trigger on a sliding scale: dividends, **AT1 coupons**, and variable compensation get progressively blocked. This is critical for AT1 economics — AT1 holders can have their coupons stopped if the bank's CET1 (not AT1) deteriorates. It's also why AT1 demands a coupon premium: the cancellation risk is real and asymmetric.

**Pillar 2**

P2R (Pillar 2 Requirement) sits between Pillar 1 and the buffers. Its composition mirrors Pillar 1: at least 56.25% CET1, at least 75% Tier 1, remainder Tier 2. So AT1 and Tier 2 can fill the same proportional slots as in Pillar 1.

P2G (Pillar 2 Guidance) sits above the buffers and must be CET1. Breaching P2G doesn't trigger MDA but triggers supervisory dialogue.

**Leverage ratio — an asymmetric constraint**

3.0% Tier 1 / total exposure (non-risk-weighted), plus a leverage buffer for G-SIBs equal to 50% of the risk-based G-SIB surcharge. **Tier 2 does not count toward the leverage ratio.** Only CET1 + AT1 do. So AT1 has dual utility (Tier 1 ratio + leverage ratio) while Tier 2 only helps the Total Capital ratio.

**Tier 2 amortizes in its last 5 years**

For regulatory recognition, Tier 2 instruments amortize straight-line over their final five years: 100% in years 5+, then 80%, 60%, 40%, 20%, 0%. A 10-year sub debt fully counts for only 5 years, then declines. This is why banks have rolling Tier 2 refinancing programs — to avoid amortization eroding the ratio.

**MREL / TLAC — the resolution stack**

For G-SIBs (TLAC: 18% of RWA / 6.75% of leverage exposure) and EU banks (MREL, bank-specific via BRRD), there's a separate eligible-liabilities requirement sitting above regulatory capital. It can be met with CET1 + AT1 + Tier 2 + eligible bail-in-able senior debt. AT1 and Tier 2 both count, but the requirement is set above the Pillar 1 capital minimum, so they're effectively doing double duty.

**No upper caps**

Under Basel III/CRR, there's no explicit cap on AT1 or Tier 2. The old Basel II rule that Tier 2 ≤ 100% of Tier 1 was removed. AT1 above 1.5% of RWA still counts toward Total Capital but provides no marginal Tier 1 benefit; same for Tier 2 above 2.0%. The constraints become economic (cost) and market-driven (investor appetite for the bank's AT1/Tier 2 paper) rather than regulatory.

**CRR III / Basel III "finalisation" overlay**

Effective in the EU from January 2025, the output floor caps the benefit of internal models: model-based RWA cannot be lower than 72.5% of the standardized approach RWA. This doesn't change the tier structure but does change the RWA denominator — which feeds back into how much AT1/Tier 2 a bank needs in absolute terms.

**Practical takeaway for capital structure**

The "binding constraint" almost always sits at CET1, because (i) buffers are CET1-only, (ii) MDA triggers on CET1, and (iii) P2G is CET1. AT1 fills a narrow Tier 1 slot with leverage-ratio utility; Tier 2 fills a narrow Total Capital slot with no leverage-ratio utility but MREL/TLAC utility. Most banks therefore size AT1 around 1.5–2% of RWA and Tier 2 around 2–3% of RWA, with the rest of the stack being CET1 plus the MREL/TLAC layer.
